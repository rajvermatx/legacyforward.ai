# What Product Managers Get Wrong About AI Features

The request comes in from sales, from leadership, from a competitor announcement: "We need an AI feature."

The PM's job — as most PMs understand it — is to figure out what that feature should do, write the requirements, and get it built. So they start writing. They schedule discovery sessions. They draft user stories. They think about the roadmap.

The one question they almost never ask is the most important one: does this actually need AI?

That question sounds obvious. It feels almost rude to ask. But skipping it is why so many AI features ship late, underperform, and quietly get deprecated eighteen months after launch. The discipline to ask it — and answer it honestly — is the difference between a PM who builds AI features and a PM who builds AI features that work.

This article is about the specific mistakes PMs make when working on AI products, and the concrete changes that fix them.

---

## The First Question: Does This Need AI at All?

Most product requirements are generated by someone observing a problem and proposing a category of solution. The category today, for almost every product and business problem, is "AI." This creates a systematic bias toward AI solutions before anyone has evaluated whether AI is the right tool.

Here is a useful diagnostic. For any proposed AI feature, ask: if the AI were removed and replaced with a deterministic rule or a simpler algorithmic approach, what would break?

If the answer is "nothing, really" — the feature could be built with rules, lookups, or basic statistics and produce equivalent value — then you are looking at an AI feature that is AI for AI's sake. That feature will be harder to build, harder to test, harder to explain to users, and harder to maintain than the simpler alternative. It will also be wrong in unpredictable ways that a rule-based system would not be. Build the simpler thing.

The cases where AI genuinely is the right tool share common characteristics: the input space is too large or variable for rules to cover, the right output depends on subtle patterns in high-dimensional data, language understanding or generation is core to the value, or the system needs to improve with use. If the proposed feature has none of these characteristics, it probably does not need AI.

This judgment call is the most valuable thing a PM brings to an AI initiative. Engineers will build what they're asked to build. Designers will design what's in scope. Stakeholders will support the roadmap item they requested. The PM is the person who looks at all of that and says: we are solving the right problem, but this is the wrong solution. Start the conversation again.

## Why Traditional User Stories Fail for Non-Deterministic Outputs

The standard Agile user story structure is: "As a [user type], I want to [action] so that [benefit]." It is followed by acceptance criteria — a list of conditions that, when verified, confirm the story is done.

This model assumes that the output of a feature is deterministic. Given input X, the feature produces output Y. The acceptance criteria describe Y. The QA engineer verifies that the feature produces Y when given X. Done.

AI features break this model at the most fundamental level. The outputs are not deterministic. Given the same input X, the feature may produce a range of outputs, some better than others, with no single correct answer. There is no "Y" to write acceptance criteria for.

The failure modes this creates are predictable.

**The "it usually works" trap.** The team demos the feature, it produces good outputs during the demo, and stakeholders accept it. QA writes a handful of test cases. Most pass. The feature ships. In production, it encounters the long tail of inputs that were never in the demo or the test suite, and produces outputs that range from mediocre to confidently wrong.

**The "make it more accurate" feedback loop.** After launch, users complain. Leadership asks the PM to improve the feature. The PM writes a ticket: "Improve model accuracy." This is not actionable. What does "accurate" mean here? Accurate on which inputs? By what measure? At what cost to performance? What is the current baseline? What is the target? The engineering team looks at the ticket, runs some evals, and reports back that accuracy improved from 73% to 76%. Is that enough? No one knows.

**The precision-recall confusion.** A feature that flags customer support tickets for escalation is being evaluated on "accuracy." But there are two very different ways to be inaccurate: flagging tickets that don't need escalation (false positives — customer service waste) and missing tickets that do need escalation (false negatives — customer harm). These have very different business costs. Traditional acceptance criteria do not capture this tradeoff. The PM who cannot talk about precision and recall cannot make product decisions in this space.

## The AI Story Template

User stories for AI features need to be structured differently. Here is the template that fixes the core problems.

**AI Story Structure:**

- **Hypothesis:** We believe that [specific AI capability] will [specific user behavior change] for [specific user segment], which will produce [specific business metric movement].
- **Input definition:** The model will operate on [specific data], in [specific context], for [specific users or events].
- **Quality threshold:** The output is acceptable when [specific metric] reaches [specific value] on [specific evaluation set]. Below this threshold, the feature should not ship.
- **Success metric:** We will know this story succeeded when [business metric] moves [direction] by [amount] within [timeframe] post-launch.
- **Kill criteria:** If the quality threshold has not been reached after [specific time or budget], we will [pivot to alternative approach / reduce scope / abandon this story].

This structure forces specificity at every point where traditional user stories allow vagueness. The hypothesis makes the causal chain explicit and testable. The quality threshold turns evaluation from a post-hoc debate into a pre-committed gate. The kill criteria establish when to stop before exploration begins.

The kill criteria are the part most PMs resist writing. It feels pessimistic. It seems to undermine confidence in the initiative. In practice, writing kill criteria before starting is one of the most valuable things a PM can do — not because it signals doubt, but because it eliminates the sunk cost logic that keeps failing initiatives running indefinitely. If we agreed before we started that three months with no approach reaching 80% precision means we pivot, we cannot pretend that the fourth month will be different.

## "Make It More Accurate" Is Not Actionable Feedback

This is worth dwelling on because it is the most common failure mode in the PM-to-engineering communication loop on AI products.

When a PM writes "improve model accuracy" or tells an engineering lead "users are saying the recommendations are not relevant, can we make them better," they have communicated a complaint, not a specification. The engineering team will make some change — retrain on more data, adjust the prompt, tune a threshold — and report that the metric improved. The cycle repeats without convergence because the target was never defined.

Actionable feedback on an AI feature specifies four things:

1. **Which metric.** Precision? Recall? F1? NDCG for ranking? Latency at p95? User satisfaction score? Each of these measures something different, and improving one may trade off against another.
2. **The current value.** If you do not have a baseline, you cannot evaluate improvement.
3. **The target value.** Where does the metric need to be for this to be working well enough?
4. **The evaluation set.** On what data are we measuring? A metric calculated on the training set is meaningless. The evaluation set should represent real production inputs, including the hard cases.

Without these four things, "improve accuracy" gives engineers permission to move any metric in any direction by any amount and report success. With them, both the PM and the engineering team are aligned on what improvement means, how it will be measured, and when the work is done.

This is not a technical skill. It is a product skill. PMs who develop it become dramatically more effective at shipping AI features that actually do what they were supposed to do.

## The AI PM Competency Map

| Traditional PM Skill | AI-Specific Extension |
|---|---|
| Writing user stories | Writing hypotheses with kill criteria |
| Defining acceptance criteria | Defining quality thresholds on evaluation sets |
| Communicating product requirements | Specifying metrics with baselines and targets |
| Managing scope | Managing the explore/deliver phase boundary |
| Measuring feature success | Monitoring for model drift post-launch |
| Prioritizing the backlog | Prioritizing based on data availability, not just business value |
| Stakeholder communication | Translating probabilistic outputs into business language |
| Handling user feedback | Distinguishing model failures from UX failures |

The right column is not a replacement for the left. It is an extension. The PMs who are most effective on AI products are not the ones who become engineers. They are the ones who understand the AI development lifecycle well enough to write specs that engineers can act on, make go/pivot/kill decisions with confidence, and communicate AI product progress to stakeholders who are used to deterministic software.

## The PM Who Changed the Question

A B2B SaaS company had a PM working on a "smart search" feature. The original brief: use AI to make the product's internal search more relevant. The PM took the brief at face value and started working on requirements for an AI-powered semantic search system.

During discovery, she asked users how they currently used search. Most told her the same thing: they didn't really use search. They used the sidebar navigation and bookmarks. The few who used search were doing it to find specific records by ID or name — a lookup task, not a semantic search task.

She asked the next question: what would make search dramatically more useful to you? The consistent answer was not "more relevant results." It was "search across everything, not just this section." Users were frustrated that search was scoped to the current module rather than the entire product.

The AI-powered semantic search system she had been scoping would have addressed neither complaint. It would have made the existing limited search more semantically sophisticated — and users would still have gotten results only from the current module, which was the actual problem.

She changed the feature. The shipped version was a cross-product search with conventional full-text search (no AI), combined with a saved-search feature that addressed the most common lookup patterns. It took six weeks to build instead of six months. Search usage tripled in the first month after launch.

The question was never "how do we make the AI better." The question was "does this need AI at all?" Asking it saved the team five months of the wrong work.
